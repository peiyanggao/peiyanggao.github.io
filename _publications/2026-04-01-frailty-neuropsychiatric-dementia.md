This study leveraged the UK Biobank cohort to investigate how physical frailty contributes to neuropsychiatric disorders through genetic, neuroanatomical, and biochemical pathways.

**Key findings:**

- Physical frailty significantly increased the risk of multiple disorders: all-cause dementia (HR = 2.14), Parkinson's disease (HR = 1.47), stroke (HR = 1.69), sleep disorders (HR = 2.63), anxiety (HR = 2.37), major depressive disorder (HR = 2.88), and schizophrenia (HR = 3.76).
- Mendelian randomization analyses suggested potential causal relationships between physical frailty and major depressive disorder (OR = 1.25) and anxiety disorder (OR = 1.30).
- Neuroimaging analyses identified structural changes in the frontal cortex and subcortical regions including the thalamus, lateral ventricle, and hippocampus.
- Peripheral biomarkers — total cholesterol, GDF15, and WFDC2 — substantially mediated the relationship between frailty and neuropsychiatric outcomes.

This work highlights physical frailty as a modifiable risk factor for a broad spectrum of neuropsychiatric disorders and reveals shared biological mechanisms involving genetics, brain structure, and peripheral inflammation.
