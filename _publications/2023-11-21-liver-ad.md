This study leveraged the CABLE cohort (N = 1,687 non-demented adults from northern China) to investigate how liver function relates to cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) biomarkers of Alzheimer's disease (AD) pathology and cognitive performance, framed within the 2018 NIA-AA AT(N) classification.

**Key findings:**

- Across AD pathological and clinical progression, levels of total protein (TP), globulin (GLO), and AST/ALT ratio increased, while albumin/globulin (A/G), albumin, prealbumin, ALT, GLDH, ADA, and AFU decreased.
- Higher TP and GLO, and lower A/G, were significantly associated with lower CSF Aβ42 and Aβ42/Aβ40, and higher T-tau/Aβ42 and P-tau/Aβ42 ratios — indicating greater AD pathology.
- Mediation analyses (10,000 bootstrap iterations) revealed that CSF Aβ42- and tau-related biomarkers partially mediated the relationship between liver function (TP, GLDH) and cognition, with mediation effects ranging from 3.91% to 16.44%.
- Findings suggest that abnormal liver function may be involved in early AD progression, and that amyloid and tau pathologies may serve as mechanistic links between peripheral liver health and cognitive decline.

This work extends prior cohort findings by integrating CSF AD biomarkers and identifies the liver as a potentially modifiable contributor to AD pathogenesis, with implications for prevention and biomarker-guided treatment strategies.
