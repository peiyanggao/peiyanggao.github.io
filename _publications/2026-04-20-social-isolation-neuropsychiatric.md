This study leveraged the UK Biobank cohort (N = 383,421) to investigate how social isolation and loneliness relate to brain health across multiple dimensions, including neurological and psychiatric disease incidence, brain structure, behavioural phenotypes, and peripheral biomarkers.

**Key findings:**

- Social isolation and loneliness were significantly associated with incident risk of 11 neurological and psychiatric disorders, including major depressive disorder, schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, anxiety, sleep disorders, dementia, Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, stroke, multiple sclerosis, and epilepsy.
- Mendelian randomization analyses provided evidence for putative causal relationships from social isolation and loneliness to major depressive disorder, schizophrenia, sleep disorders, and epilepsy.
- Both exposures were associated with worse cognitive and emotional performance, as well as structural alterations in multiple brain regions.
- Mediation analyses revealed that peripheral inflammatory and biochemical markers partially mediated the relationships between social factors and neuropsychiatric outcomes.

This work reinforces social isolation and loneliness as critical, modifiable risk factors for brain health and provides multi-dimensional evidence — epidemiological, genetic, neuroimaging, and biomarker — for their broad impact on the nervous system.
