This study leveraged the UK Biobank cohort (N = 274,194) to investigate the long-term relationship between physical frailty, genetic predisposition, and incident dementia, with particular emphasis on their joint effect.

**Key findings:**

- Compared to non-frail participants, the hazard ratio (HR) for dementia incidence was 1.40 (95% CI: 1.29–1.51) in pre-frail individuals and 2.30 (95% CI: 2.03–2.62) in frail individuals.
- Compared to participants with no frailty and low polygenic risk score (PRS), those with frailty and high PRS had a nearly 4-fold higher dementia risk (HR = 3.91, 95% CI: 3.05–5.01).
- The combined effect of physical frailty and genetic risk was monotonic — increasing frailty status with increasing PRS yielded progressively higher dementia incidence.
- Frailty modification (e.g., improving grip strength, increasing physical activity, reducing exhaustion) may serve as a viable strategy for early dementia prevention, particularly in individuals at high genetic risk.

This work supports the role of physical frailty as an actionable, modifiable target for dementia prevention, and demonstrates that genetic risk does not preclude the benefits of lifestyle-based interventions.
